What Happens When You Let a Grid Compose Your Music

A look at cellular automata as a tool for automated composition

When I first encountered cellular automata as an option for this project, I understood the name and not much else. I picked it anyway — and what followed was probably the most unexpectedly engaging rabbit hole I've gone down. Before writing a single line of code I spent hours watching videos and reading about how these simulations work, not because I had to, but because I genuinely couldn't stop.

What is a cellular automaton?

A cellular automaton is a grid of cells, each either alive (on) or dead (off). At every step — called a generation — each cell looks at its eight neighbors and decides whether to live or die based on a simple ruleset. The most famous version is Conway's Game of Life, whose rules are:

A live cell with 2 or 3 live neighbors survives. A dead cell with exactly 3 live neighbors becomes alive. Everything else dies or stays dead.

That's it. Three rules. And yet from those three rules emerge gliders that travel across the grid, oscillators that pulse in place, and stable structures that sit completely still while chaos unfolds around them. None of this behavior is programmed in — it all emerges on its own, which is what makes cellular automata so fascinating to watch.

How the simulation maps to music

The core idea is simple: treat the grid as a musical score. Each row corresponds to a pitch, and each generation corresponds to a beat in time. At every beat, the code checks which cells in the leftmost column are alive, and plays those notes simultaneously using the Web Audio API. Low rows play low notes, high rows play high notes.

The audio scheduling itself is handled by the Web Audio API's internal clock via AudioContext.currentTime. Rather than triggering notes inside a setInterval loop — which drifts — all 40 beats are scheduled up front at precise timestamps. Web Audio fires them with sample-accurate timing regardless of what else the browser is doing.

The density problem I didn't expect

One surprising discovery: starting density directly affects how long the piece lasts. Pack the initial grid too densely and the Game of Life rules cause an immediate population collapse — too many neighbors means too many cells die in the first few generations, and the grid goes quiet fast. A sparse grid stabilizes into patterns that persist much longer. So the density control ended up being less about "more notes versus fewer notes" and more about "how quickly does this piece burn out." That felt like a genuine musical insight that came directly from understanding the simulation.

How different combinations feel

The tool has three controls: oscillator type (the waveform shape — sine, square, sawtooth, triangle), scale (pentatonic, minor, major, chromatic, whole tone), and density. They interact in interesting ways. A sine wave over a pentatonic scale at low density sounds almost ambient and peaceful. Switch to sawtooth with the chromatic scale at high density and it becomes genuinely chaotic — harsh, clashing, unpredictable. The square wave feels retro, which suits the pixelated grid aesthetic well.

Does it feel like music?

Honestly — not in the way music made by people feels. There's no melody, no tension building toward resolution, no sense that any note was chosen. Knowing the output comes from a grid simulation makes it hard to hear as expressive. But it's not nothing either. There's a texture to it, an automated electronic quality with its own character. Some combinations produce something that feels almost rhythmic, almost intentional — and that near-miss quality is what makes it interesting. Algorithms can't compose the way people do, but they produce things people never would, and sometimes that's enough to be worth listening to.
